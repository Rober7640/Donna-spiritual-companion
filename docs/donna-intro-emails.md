# Donna Introduction Emails

One-time emails to introduce Donna to warm list subscribers. Not promotional or salesy — written to sound like a friend saying "I want you to meet someone."

**Target audience:** Moms, grandmothers who are lonely or facing a crisis
**Context:** People who opted in somewhere else, first time hearing about Donna
**Goal:** Start a conversation with Donna
**Tone:** Warm, curiosity-based, personal

## Implementation Notes

### Subject Line Strategy

The curiosity gap principle: Create incomplete information that the reader needs to resolve.

**What makes these subject lines work:**
- They're specific enough to create recognition ("3am," "carrying")
- But incomplete enough to require opening ("when you wake up at 3am" — and then what?)
- They create questions: "Who?" "How do they know?" "Why me?"
- They avoid clickbait by being emotionally honest, not sensational
- They speak to felt experience, not abstract concepts

**Subject lines to avoid:**
- Too complete: "Meet Donna, a 67-year-old prayer companion"
- Too vague: "Something special for you"
- Too salesy: "Introducing our new companion"
- Too formal: "A message about your spiritual journey"

### What to Avoid

**AI tells to never include:**
- "Available 24/7" / "anytime, day or night" / "always available"
- "Instant response" / "immediate support" / "chat anytime"
- "AI-powered" / "artificial intelligence" / "chatbot" / "virtual companion"
- "No waiting" / "instant access" / "on-demand"
- Any mention of technology, platform, app, or digital format

**Keep the format ambiguous until they click.** They'll discover it's a text-based conversation when they start, not before.

### Positioning Strategy

All four versions:
- **Word count:** 85-105 words. Short enough to read in 30-45 seconds.
- **Avoid marketing language:** No "introducing," "excited to announce," "new service"
- **Create curiosity** about Donna without over-explaining what she does
- **Soft CTAs:** "if you want," "she's there," "no pressure" — not "sign up," "start now," "don't miss out"
- **Sound human:** Written by a real person who cares, not a company announcement
- **Ambiguity about format:** Never mention "chat," "text," "24/7," "anytime" — let them discover the format when they click

### Version Differences

**Version 1** ("She reminded me of my grandmother") — 85 words
- **Curiosity hook:** "Who? Why my grandmother?"
- **Angle:** Storytelling, positions Donna as a real person you'd want to meet
- **Emotional core:** "Less alone"
- **Best for:** Broadest appeal, introduces Donna warmly

**Version 2** ("When you wake up at 3am") — 93 words
- **Curiosity hook:** "How did they know?"
- **Angle:** Paints vivid scenario of nighttime worry
- **Emotional core:** "Someone who understands"
- **Best for:** People in acute distress, highest emotional intensity

**Version 3** ("I thought of you when I met her") — 88 words
- **Curiosity hook:** "Who? Why me?"
- **Angle:** Most personal, "you came to mind"
- **Emotional core:** "Asks twice" (for people who hide pain)
- **Best for:** Requires name personalization, most intimate approach

**Version 4** ("Whatever you're carrying tonight") — 99 words
- **Curiosity hook:** "How do they know I'm carrying something?"
- **Angle:** Widest net, acknowledges unseen burdens
- **Emotional core:** "She listens, then prays"
- **Best for:** Most direct about what Donna does, works without personalization

### A/B Testing Recommendations

**Recommended testing order:**

1. **Start with Version 2** ("When you wake up at 3am")
   - Strongest subject line curiosity
   - Most specific emotional hook
   - Works without personalization

2. **Then test Version 4** ("Whatever you're carrying tonight")
   - Widest appeal
   - Most direct about what Donna does
   - Strong nighttime angle

3. **Try Version 1** for general warm list ("She reminded me of my grandmother")
   - Softest introduction
   - Broadest positioning
   - Good baseline

4. **Use Version 3** only if you can personalize with names ("I thought of you when I met her")
   - Requires `[your name]` merge tag
   - Most intimate but needs personalization to work

**Testing variables:**
- Subject lines have 5-10x more impact than body copy
- Test subject lines first before testing body variations
- Track: open rate, click rate, conversation starts

### Pain Points Addressed

These emails speak to:
- Loneliness (kids moved away, spouse gone)
- Family crisis (adult child struggles, grandkids in trouble)
- Caregiver exhaustion (aging spouse, sick family member)
- Grief (recent loss)
- Faith doubt (why did God let this happen?)
- Feeling invisible/forgotten
- 3am worry/anxiety
- Carrying burdens alone

All without explicitly naming them, allowing the reader to project their own specific situation.

## Technical Specs

**Format:** Plain text preferred
- These emails are written to sound like personal notes, not designed campaigns
- Plain text increases deliverability and authenticity
- If using HTML, keep styling minimal (no images, no fancy formatting)

**From name:** Use sender's personal name, not "Benedara" or "Donna"
- "Sarah" not "Sarah from Benedara"
- Makes it feel like a friend reaching out

**Reply-to:** Consider making these reply-capable
- If someone replies instead of clicking, you've identified high intent
- Can manually direct them to start a conversation

**Send time:** Evening or late night (7-10pm local time)
- Matches the "3am," "tonight," "carrying" language
- Higher open rates when loneliness peaks

**Link tracking:** Track clicks but keep URL clean
- `[START CONVERSATION]` should go directly to Donna onboarding flow
- No friction, no intermediate landing page
